Overview

Quiche is a french tart consisting of pastry crust filled with savoury custard and pieces of cheese, meat, seafood, or vegetables. A well-known variant is quiche lorraine which includes lardons or bacons. Quiche may be served hot, warm or cold.

History

Quiche is a french dish originating from the eastern part of the country; however using eggs and cream in pasrty has a wider history. It was practiced in Italian cuisine at least as early as the 13th century. Recipes for eggs and cream baked in pastry containing meat, fish and fruit are reffered to as crustardes in the 14th-century The Forme of Cury and in 15th-century cookbooks, such as the Italian Libro de Arte Coquinaria.

American food writter, James Peterson records first encountering quiche in the late 1960s and being convinced it was the most delicious thing I'd ever tatsted. He writes that by the 1980s American quiches began to include ingredients that he found bizarre and unpleasant, such as brocoli, and that he regards Bruce Feirstein's 1982 comedy book Real Men don't Eat Quiche as the final humiliation of the dish that a rugged and honest country dish had become a symbol of effete snobbery.

Variety

A quiche usually has a pastry crust and a filling of eggs and milk and/or cream. It may be made with vegetables, meat or seafood, and be served hot, warm or cold. Here are some types of quiches:

Quiche aux Camembert:

Ingredients: Camembert cheese, cream, eggs

Quiche aux Champignons:

Ingredients: Mushrooms, cream, eggs

Quiche aux Fromage de Gruyere:

Ingredients: Gruyere cheese, cream, eggs, bacon

Quiche Lorraine:

Ingredients: Cream, eggs, bacon
